Let's talk about sentences.

When well-constructed, sentences appear effortless. Their intrusion becomes so subtle that they dissolve into the substrate of our consciousness. You are not reading them; you are //discovering// them. You and the author are co-conspirators, collaborating to explore a world that existed long before it was written down. But how do writers achieve this? 

Simple: by understanding and developing the principal elements of plain, clear, effective sentence composition. 

This article aims to introduce you to some of these elements through the construct of clauses and the four sentence types. It draws heavily on [http://www.chompchomp.com/menu.htm Grammar Bytes!] (an online resource for grammar instruction) and the 2008 edition of //[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style Elements of Style]// by White and Strunk (revised by John Woldemar Cowan; available  [http://vrici.lojban.org/~cowan/style-revised.html here]). It also includes numerous flourishes unique to this community and a few observations of my own.

Keep in mind: This is not a comprehensive guide to grammar. This is only an explanation of one aspect of grammar: Clauses, and how you can connect them to create simple, compound, complex, and complex-compound sentences. 

Let's begin.

+ What is a Sentence?

A complete sentence starts with a capital letter and ends with terminal punctuation (<<{{**.**}}>>, <<{{**!**}}>>, or <<{{**?**}}>>). It contains at least one main (or **independent**) clause, and any number of subordinate (or **dependent**) clauses.

+ What is a Clause?
A clause is two or more words that declare something about something.

Every clause contains two components: the **subject** and the **predicate**. The subject is the person or thing about whom the declaration is about; the predicate is what is being declared about it. 

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][SUBJECT][[/span]] +  [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][PREDICATE][[/span]] = CLAUSE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]exist[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]Julia[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]likes cake[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]must gather more bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

A  **simple subject** is the noun that the predicate is addressing: **Your squishy, misshapen [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]meat-sack[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]is full of bones[[/span]].**

A **complete subject** is the same noun, plus any modifiers which qualify it: **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]Your squishy, misshapen meat-sack[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]is full of bones[[/span]].**

For our purposes, assume that the term 'subject' refers to the //complete// subject.

++ Independent 
If a clause is **independent**, it can function as a complete sentence. It has a subject, a predicate, and is a complete declaration (a finished thought).

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][SUBJECT][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][PREDICATE][[/span]] = INDEPENDENT**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]are here[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]Jill[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]is watching her friends[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]Everyone's bones[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]keep disappearing[[/span]].
[[/div]]

A complete sentence //always// contains at least one independent clause.

++ Dependent

If a clause is **dependent** (or **subordinate**), it cannot function as a complete sentence. It has a subject and a predicate, but it is introduced with a **subordinating conjunction**[[footnote]]This is not //always// true. **Relative clauses** are dependent clauses which are introduced with either a **relative pronoun** or **relative adverb**; however, these are beyond the scope of this article. If you'd like to read more, I'd recommend [http://www.chompchomp.com/terms/relativeclause.htm this] article by Grammar Bytes.[[/footnote]]. It is an incomplete declaration (an unfinished thought):

> = **[[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][SUBJECT][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][PREDICATE][[/span]] = DEPENDENT**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]If[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]we[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]are here[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]Why[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]Jill[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]is watching her friends[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]Whether or not[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]everyone's bones[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]keep disappearing[[/span]].
[[/div]]

Subordinating conjunctions will be explained later (along with conjunctions). For now, just remember that when a clause starts with certain words or phrases (such as <<**{{but}}**>>, <<**{{why}}**>>, <<**{{whose}}**>>, <<**{{in order that}}**>>, or <<{{**because**}}>>) it becomes 'subordinate' (or dependent) to an independent clause.

+ Sentence Types
There are four types of sentences: **simple**, **compound**, **complex**, and **compound-complex**.

++ Simple
Simple sentences consist of one independent clause and no dependent clauses:
> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = SIMPLE SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We stay[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]].
[[/div]]

Simple sentences can begin with a **coordinating conjunction**:

> = **[[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][COORD. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = SIMPLE SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]But[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]we stay[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]So[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]my dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]And[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]].
[[/div]]

However, it is typical for a previous sentence to contextualize the conjunction:
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* They tell us to leave. [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]But[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]we stay[[/span]].
* I gave him all of my bones. [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]So[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]my dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]].
* I've been out all day. [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]And[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]].
[[/div]]

There are seven coordinating conjunctions: <<**{{and}}**>>, <<**{{but}}**>>, <<**{{for}}**>>, <<**{{nor}}**>>, <<**{{or}}**>>, <<**{{so}}**>>, and <<**{{yet}}**>>. You can use one with a comma to join two independent clauses together, or by itself to introduce an independent clause. 

Conjunctions establish a relationship between clauses, binding them together. Think of them like grammatical glue. 

And you know what glue is made of, right? ~:o)

++ Compound
Compound sentences consist of two or more independent clauses and no dependent clauses. You can use a comma and a coordinating conjunction to make one:

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][COORD. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPOUND SENTENCE**
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We stay[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], and [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]they die[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], so [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I gave him more bones[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], yet [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I forgot the bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

Compound sentences can also be made with just a semicolon, but only if the relationship between them is clear and implicit:
> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][;][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPOUND SENTENCE**
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We stay[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]they die[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I gave him more bones[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I forgot the bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

You can also make one with a semicolon, **conjunctive adverb**, and a comma:

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][;][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][CONJ. ADVERB][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPOUND SENTENCE**
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We stay[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; therefore, [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]they die[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; finally, [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I told him to stop[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; however, [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I forgot the bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

A conjunctive adverb can also be used to introduce, interrupt, or conclude an independent clause:
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]Therefore,[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]we stay[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], meanwhile,[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]dug a very deep hole[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon from the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], however[[/span]].
[[/div]]

In this case, the conjunctive adverb only needs to be segregated with commas if its intrusion in an independent clause is disruptive.

++ Complex
Complex sentences consist of one independent clause and one (or more) dependent clauses. The simplest way to make one is to use a **subordinating conjunction**. If a dependent clause precedes an independent clause, use a comma to link the two:

> = **[[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]][DEP. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPLEX SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]If [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]]we stay[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], [[/span]][[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]they die[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]When [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]]my dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], [[/span]][[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]I yelled at him[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]Because [[/span]][[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon at the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], [[/span]][[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]I have plenty of food[[/span]].
[[/div]]

If a dependent clause comes //after// the independent clause, omit the comma: 

> = **[[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]][DEP. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPLEX SENTENCE**


[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]We stay[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]if[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #80ff80;"]]they die[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]when[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #80ff80;"]]I yelled at him[[/span]].
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon at the store[[/span]]  [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]because[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #80ff80"]]I had no food[[/span]].
[[/div]]

++ Compound-Complex
Compound-Complex sentences consist of two or more independent clauses and one or more dependent clauses. A compound-complex sentence can use subordinate, coordinate, or adverbial conjunctions. 

Here are some examples using what we've learned so far:

> = **[[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]][DEP. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][;][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][CONJ. ADVERB][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPLEX SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]If[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]]we stay[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], [[/span]][[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]they die[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; therefore,[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]we must go[[/span]].
[[/div]]

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][COOR. CONJ][[/span]]  + [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]][DEP. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPLEX SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]My dog dug a very deep hole[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], and[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #b3d1ff;"]]I wasn't happy about it[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]until[[/span]]  [[span style="background-color:#80ff80;"]]I gave him all of my bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][;][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]][SUB. CONJ.][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #80ff80;"]][DEP. CLAUSE][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][IND. CLAUSE][[/span]] = COMPLEX SENTENCE**

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I'll be fine[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]];[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#ffb3ff;"]]because[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #80ff80;"]]I picked up eggs, milk, and bacon at the store[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]],[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]I have no bones[[/span]].
[[/div]]

+ Extra Credit

++ Sequences
A **sequence** is a series of two or more terms. If there are three or more terms, use a comma after each term except the last (which is always preceded by a coordinating conjunction like <<**{{and}}>>** or <<**{{or}}**>>).

> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][TERM][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][COOR. CONJ][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][TERM][[/span]]  = 2-TERM SEQUENCE**
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* The children brought [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]femurs[[/span]] [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]and[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]skulls[[/span]].
[[/div]]
> = **[[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][TERM][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][TERM][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][,][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]][COOR. CONJ][[/span]] + [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]][TERM][[/span]]  = 3-TERM SEQUENCE**
[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* The children brought [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]femurs[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]],[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]skulls[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]], and[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]teeth[[/span]].
[[/div]]

When the terms use commas internally, use semicolons in lieu of commas to separate them: 

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* The children brought [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]femurs, from their fathers[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]];[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]skulls, from their mothers[[/span]][[span style="background-color: #ffe066;"]]; and[[/span]] [[span style="background-color:#b3d1ff;"]]teeth, torn from sisters and brothers[[/span]].

[[/div]]

The last comma in a sequence (prior to <<**{{and}}**>>) is referred to as an Oxford, Harvard, or [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_comma serial comma]; it is optional. However, its exclusion can drastically alter how a sentence is understood. For example:

[[div style="font-weight: bold;"]]
* They were buried with their siblings, a cat, and a dog.
* They were buried with their siblings, [who are] a cat and a dog.
[[/div]]
